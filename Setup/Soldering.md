# Soldering Equipment

A usable electronics soldering setup needs temperature control, suitable tips,
fume capture, a stable stand, ESD awareness, and safe consumable storage. A cheap iron
that overheats boards and has no safe stand costs more than it saves.

## Minimum kit

- Temperature-controlled station with replaceable tips
- Grounded or verified ESD-safe iron suitable for sensitive electronics
- Stable stand with brass wool and damp sponge
- Medium chisel tip plus smaller tip for fine work
- Electronics-grade solder and compatible flux
- Solder wick and a manual desoldering pump
- Fine flush cutters, tweezers, and small pliers
- PCB holder, eye protection, and local fume extraction
- Heat-resistant work area and a safe place for hot tips

## Station checklist

- Correct 220-240 V input and Malaysian-compatible plug/fuse arrangement
- Protective-earth continuity where the design requires grounding
- No exposed mains terminals or loose metal enclosure
- Fast recovery and enough power for ground planes without excessive set temperature
- Sleep/standby and automatic shutoff preferred
- Readily available genuine or compatible tips with known fit
- Silicone or heat-resistant iron cable
- Replaceable heater/handpiece and documented calibration where possible

Do not use a station with a damaged cable, loose earth, cracked handpiece, unstable
stand, or suspicious mains wiring. Have mains equipment inspected by a competent
person; do not learn mains repair by opening a plugged-in soldering station.

## Technique and temperature

Use the largest practical tip that contacts both surfaces, keep it clean and tinned,
and heat the joint rather than melting solder onto the tip. Start with the solder and
flux manufacturer's recommended temperature. Increase temperature only when tip size,
thermal mass, oxidation, or dwell time has been addressed.

Excess temperature accelerates tip oxidation, burns flux, damages pads, and creates
more fume. The HSE recommends never overheating the iron, keeping the head out of the
plume, and using extraction. See [Fume extractors](Fume-Extractors.md).

## Consumables

- Use electronics solder with a known alloy and flux type.
- Do not use plumbing acid flux on electronics.
- Keep lead-containing solder labelled and separate; wash hands after handling it.
- Lead-free does not mean fume-free; flux still requires exposure control.
- Store liquid flux and cleaners closed, labelled, and according to their SDS.
- Use a practice PCB before repairing irreplaceable hardware.

## Shutdown checklist

1. Clean and tin the tip.
2. Return the iron fully into its stand.
3. Switch off and allow it to cool unattended only in a clear, non-combustible area.
4. Close flux and solvent containers.
5. Dispose of solder waste and contaminated wipes appropriately.
6. Wash hands before eating or touching the face.

## Maintenance stock

Keep these consumables before starting repair work:

- Fluxed desoldering wick in several useful widths, stored sealed and dry
- A manual solder sucker with a replacement seal/nozzle if available
- Brass wool refill and cellulose sponge
- At least one general-purpose chisel tip and one spare tip used by the station
- Known-alloy electronics solder and compatible flux
- Tip tinner/cleaner used sparingly according to its instructions
- Heat-shrink, wire, headers, and strain relief
- A safe backup iron or compatible replacement handpiece for time-critical work

Do not scrape a plated soldering tip with a file or abrasive to extend its life. If a
tip no longer wets after correct cleaning/tinning, replace it. Record the exact tip
series and handpiece model; visually similar tips can have incompatible sensors,
heaters, dimensions, or grounding.
